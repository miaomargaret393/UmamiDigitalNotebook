As part of Princeton's Mechanical Engineering program (minors in Bioengineering and Materials Science Engineering), my undergrad senior thesis — Topographic Control of Cell Migration: A Nanofabricated 3D Imaging Platform for Biomaterial and Device Interface Engineering — focused on building a 3D biocompatible micro-scale platform to study how cells respond to extreme geometries and how they conform to them.

This falls under contact guidance, a part of mechanotransduction where substrate geometry is used to guide cell migration and probe behavior. Past research mostly looked at grooves and smooth concave/convex surfaces, but nobody had really compared rapidly ascending gradients, sharp edges, and geometric singularities (super sharp tips).

On top of that, imaging 3D cell models at high magnification (40x+) is already hard, and doing it on complex geometry makes it even harder. Using the Nanoscribe for high-fidelity printing and PDMS replication molding, I fabricated and iterated chips with precise geometric features, then analyzed how the cell membrane and nucleus deformed and traveled across these high-curvature surfaces.
