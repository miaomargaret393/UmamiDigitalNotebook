This project, Quantifying Metal-Organic Framework (MOF) CO2 Adsorption Thermal Dynamics, was done as a part of CBE423/MSE 423: Computational Materials Design and Discovery's final project, which consisted of a self-led computational project to investigate a research question regarding the unmet gaps that computational material discovery needs.

For the longest time, people have been using static density functional theory (DFT) to model how CO2 binds to metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) — porous, tunable materials that are a big deal for carbon capture. DFT is accurate but expensive, and it only really gives you a snapshot at 0K, so it can't capture how binding actually behaves once you add realistic temperatures and motion into the picture. Molecular dynamics (MD) can simulate that thermal behavior over time, but running it with full DFT accuracy is very slow, which is where machine-learned interatomic potentials (MLIPs) come in! They're trained on huge DFT datasets and let you get DFT-level accuracy at a fraction of the compute cost.

In this project, we used the UMA-ODAC MLIP (trained specifically with MOFs) to study CO2 adsorption in Mg-MOF-74, a well-studied carbon capture MOF, first calculating static binding energies across all its metal sites, then running MD simulations from 300K to 1000K to watch how the CO2's binding behavior changed with temperature. We found that as temperature increases, CO2 starts "hopping" between metal sites instead of staying put, and that this desorption is driven mainly by the molecule drifting away rather than any real change in the binding geometry itself, a trend that lined up nicely with real experimental desorption temperatures.

This project is done in collaboration with Chris Li (CEE '26) and Brendan Archer (MAE'26).
